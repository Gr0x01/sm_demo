# What SEC Filings Reveal About Upgrade Revenue in Homebuilding

Upgrade revenue accounts for 8–25% of average selling price among public homebuilders. It carries a 3–5 point margin premium over the base house. And there is no current industry benchmark for it. The most-cited source on design center upgrade spending is a ProBuilder survey from 2007. Nothing comprehensive has been published since.

We went through filings and earnings disclosures from 14 publicly traded builders to pull out what we could about this revenue line. The picture that came back: a category that nobody in the industry talks about in benchmarking, despite it being the most margin-rich line on the P&L.

---

## What the Filings Show

Of 14 public builders we reviewed, only two disclose upgrade and options revenue at a level useful for benchmarking: Toll Brothers and PulteGroup.

**Toll Brothers (Luxury, FY2024)**
10,813 homes delivered. ~$977K ASP. $203,000 in design studio upgrades, structural options, and lot premiums per home, or 20.8% of ASP. In FY2023, when spec mix was lower, per-home options hit $236K (22.9% of ASP).

That's $2.2 billion in implied upgrade revenue in FY2024 alone. CEO Doug Yearley has called the design studios "highly accretive," noting they generate over $1 billion in annual sales.

**PulteGroup (Move-Up, Q2 2024)**
$549K ASP. $104,000 in options and lot premiums per home. 18.9% of ASP.

**The other 12 builders** (D.R. Horton, Lennar, NVR, KB Home, Meritage, Taylor Morrison, Tri Pointe, Century Communities, M/I Homes, Smith Douglas, Dream Finders, Beazer) do not disclose upgrade revenue separately. They roll it into total homebuilding revenue with no breakout.

The industry's most margin-rich revenue stream is invisible in public financial reporting.

*(Note on Lennar: their "Everything's Included" model bundles many upgrades into the base price, so direct comparison isn't possible. They embed upgrade revenue into ASP rather than reporting it separately. Different strategy entirely.)*

---

## Process Determines Revenue

The most revealing number in Toll Brothers' disclosures isn't the $203K headline. It's what happens when you split spec vs. build-to-order.

Between FY2023 and FY2024, Toll's spec mix rose from 27% to 49% of deliveries. Per-home options dropped from $236K to $203K over the same period. Spec buyers spend less in the Design Studio. That's not surprising. What's worth paying attention to is how much less.

Yearley stated on earnings calls that spec home margins run roughly 200–250 basis points below the company average. BTO homes with full Design Studio engagement hold adjusted gross margins above 30%. Most of that gap traces back to Design Studio revenue.

Same builder. Same communities. Same base prices. Wildly different upgrade revenue depending on whether the buyer walks through a design studio or walks into a finished home. Upgrade revenue isn't about buyer wealth or home price. It's about how the builder runs the selection process.

---

## Benchmark Ranges by Segment

Combining the disclosed numbers with industry surveys and a few regional builder case studies, the ranges shake out roughly like this:

- **Luxury / Semi-Custom:** 20–25%+ of ASP (including structural options and lot premiums)
- **Move-Up:** 12–20% of ASP (design center-driven)
- **Entry-Level / Production:** 8–15% of ASP (limited selections)
- **Weighted Average (design center only):** 10–15% of ASP

These are directional, not precise. That's part of the point: the data to build precise benchmarks doesn't exist publicly.

One caveat: Toll Brothers' figures include structural options and lot premiums alongside design studio selections. Pure design center spend (countertops, flooring, fixtures, smart home) is a lower share of ASP, though no builder breaks that out separately.

---

## What This Means in Dollars

For a move-up builder with a $400K ASP, the difference between capturing 12% and 20% of ASP in upgrade revenue is $32,000 per home.

Across 200 closings, that's $6.4 million in annual revenue. At margins 3–5 points above the base house, the gross profit difference is roughly $2.5–3.5M.

That's within the same segment. Not luxury vs. entry-level. Just the spread between typical and optimized within the move-up category.

## The Buyer Psychology

In 2020, Meritage Homes commissioned a study through Wakefield Research. 45% of new home buyers exceeded their upgrade budget, overspending by an average of $21,000. One in four found the process stressful. 37% said their finished homes didn't turn out as expected.

Meritage's response was to redesign the process from scratch. They cut design center time from 20+ hours per buyer to about 3 hours using curated collections. The idea: a faster, less stressful process could maintain upgrade revenue while reducing friction and regret.

Then there's retention. Toll Brothers runs a 2.3% cancellation rate, roughly half the industry average. Yearley attributes part of that to the attachment buyers build through the design studio process. Once you've spent hours picking finishes and making a home yours, you're less inclined to walk away.

Both Toll and Tri Pointe have said on earnings calls that buyers prefer incentive dollars directed toward design studio upgrades over mortgage rate reductions. The upgrade experience becomes its own form of switching cost.

## Why This Data Doesn't Exist

Three reasons:

**SEC disclosure standards don't require it.** Options and upgrades aren't a separately reportable segment under GAAP. Builders fold it into "homebuilding revenue" with no obligation to break it out.

**Competitive sensitivity.** Option pricing, attach rates, and design studio profitability are some of the most closely guarded numbers in the industry.

**No standard taxonomy.** "Lot premiums" can mean community premiums, homesite premiums, or view premiums. "Structural options" can mean elevation changes, room additions, or garage expansions. Nobody agrees on definitions, which makes comparison hard even where data does exist.

---

## The Implied Market

At a conservative 10% weighted average, options and upgrades are a $12+ billion revenue category among the top 10 public builders alone (~$120B in combined revenue). At 15%, it's $18B. At 20%, $24B.

That's just the public builders. There are roughly 44,000 private builders in the U.S. And unlike base home pricing, which is boxed in by appraisals, comparables, and competitive pressure, upgrade revenue responds directly to how well the builder presents the selection experience.

---

## What This Means for Private Builders

Private builders don't file with the SEC. They also don't benchmark upgrade revenue against peers, because there hasn't been a peer dataset to benchmark against.

A few things stood out to us in the data:

**The range within each segment is wider than most builders think.** Robino-Corrozi, a ~300-home regional builder in Dover, DE, reported that even their lower-end buyers spent 15–25% of selling price on upgrades. Adding a formal design center increased option revenue by about 10% over model-home-only sales.

**Process matters more than price point.** The Toll spec vs. BTO numbers make this hard to argue with. A private builder doing 100 homes at $400K who moves from 10% to 16% in upgrade capture adds $2.4M in annual revenue at above-average margins.

**The most-cited benchmark is 19 years old.** ProBuilder's 2007 survey is still the industry's go-to reference. Buyer expectations, design studio operations, and the universe of available upgrades look nothing like they did in 2007.

**Upgrade investment correlates with retention.** For a builder doing 200 homes a year, cutting cancellation rates from 15% to 10% avoids 10 lost sales and all the remarketing, carrying costs, and community disruption that come with them.
