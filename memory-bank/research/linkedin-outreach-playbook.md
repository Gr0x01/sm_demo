# LinkedIn Outreach Playbook

## Golden Rule
The connection request is NOT the pitch. It's the door. Walk through it empty-handed. Introduce the tool in the REPLY after they connect.

## What Works
- Ask a real question about THEIR process
- Reference something specific you noticed (community name, design center photo, recent award, floor plan)
- Make it about their world, not your product
- End on the question — let the question BE the close

## What Doesn't Work
- Leading with the product description
- Copy-pasting the same core sentence with name/company swaps
- "Would love to get your take" as a verbal tic on every message
- Generic qualifiers like "with your sales background" that apply to anyone
- Inventing shared connections or details (DON'T say you're local, went to their school, etc. unless it's TRUE)

## The Template Trap
If you can swap company names and the message still works with zero other changes, it's a template. Builders have been pitched their whole careers — they can feel the difference. Five well-researched messages that start real conversations > twenty templates that get polite ignores.

## Five Angles to Rotate

### 1. The Question (no pitch)
Ask about their current process. No mention of the tool. Best for design center managers and online sales roles.
> "Hi [Name], how does [Company] handle design selections right now? Sample boards and in-person appointments, or do you have anything online for buyers?"

### 2. The Observation
Notice something specific about their company. Tie the outreach to that observation.
> "Hi [Name], [Company]'s design studio looks great. Curious how buyers share their selections with family after the appointment, or preview options before they come in."

### 3. The Tinkerer Intro
"I've been building something..." but make the WHY specific to their world. Best for CEOs/presidents.
> "Hi [Name], your homes in [location] are well done. I've been building a tool that shows buyers what their upgrade picks look like together in actual room photos — instead of imagining it from individual samples. Would love to get your take if you're open."

### 4. The Shared Context
Reference a market condition, trend, or something from a builder publication. Best for VPs and division-level.
> "Hi [Name], I saw that [specific thing] and it made me think about [related problem]. Curious how [Company] approaches that."

### 5. The Proof Point (use later, once you have traction)
Reference conversations with other builders.
> "Hi [Name], I showed this to a builder in [market] and they said X. I'm curious if you'd see it differently."

## Closes (rotate these too)
- "Happy to show you what it looks like if you're curious."
- "No pitch — just like talking to people who actually run this process."
- "Would be great to hear how [Company] thinks about this."
- Or just end on the question. No additional ask needed.

## Pre-Message Checklist (90 seconds)
Before writing each message, find ONE specific thing:
- [ ] A community name from their website
- [ ] A design center photo from their Instagram
- [ ] A floor plan name
- [ ] A quote from a press release
- [ ] A recent LinkedIn post or award
- [ ] Their Zillow listing showing standard vs. upgrade finishes

That one detail is the difference between "this person looked at my company" and "this is a LinkedIn pitch."

## Who to Target (priority order)
1. **Design Studio/Center Manager** — lives the daily pain
2. **Online Sales Manager/Executive** — closing remotely, feels the gap
3. **VP/Director of Sales & Marketing** — owns the number, can champion internally
4. **VP of Design** — owns the selection experience
5. **President/CEO** — decision-maker, but only if the company is small enough that they're hands-on

## Who to Skip
- Purchasing Managers (supply side, not buyer side)
- VP of Operations / Construction (build side, not sales side)
- CFO/Controller (finance brain, not experience brain)
- Community Sales Managers (too junior to champion a new tool)
- Anyone with < 50 LinkedIn connections or zero activity

## Deprioritize
- Builders already on Envision Options or Roomored — they feel "solved" even though they're not. Come back with case studies.
- Builders acquired by nationals (D.R. Horton, Clayton/Berkshire) — corporate procurement is slow.

## The Research → Message Process (Step by Step)

This is how we actually craft each message. Takes 5-10 minutes per builder. The research IS the message — you're not writing copy, you're finding the one thing that makes it real.

### Step 1: Pick the Right Person (2 min)
Go to the builder's LinkedIn company page → People tab. Look for:
- VP of Sales & Marketing (ideal — owns the number AND the experience)
- Design Studio/Center Manager (feels the daily pain)
- President/CEO (only if small builder where they're hands-on)

**Red flags to skip someone:**
- Their primary role is at a different company (contractor/agency, not full-time)
- Zero posts and < 100 connections (not active on LinkedIn)
- Title is operations, construction, purchasing, or finance
- Too junior to champion anything internally

If there's a new VP (< 1 year in role), prioritize them — new leaders want quick wins.

### Step 2: Research the Builder (3-5 min)
Check these sources for ONE specific detail:
1. **Their LinkedIn activity** — recent posts, shared articles, awards, hiring
2. **Their website** — community names, design center photos, buyer journey page, included features
3. **Google "[Builder Name] design center"** or "[Builder Name] design studio" — see what comes up
4. **The contact's personal LinkedIn** — career history, past roles, anything that reveals what they care about
5. **Awards/press** — OBIE, Nationals, HBA, ProBuilder rankings

You're looking for ONE of these:
- A specific community name + what makes it notable
- A design center/studio detail (or the absence of one)
- A recent post they made about a specific topic
- Something from their career history that connects to design/visualization
- A campaign, award, or initiative they're proud of

### Step 3: Write the Message (2 min)
Formula: **[Specific observation] → [Genuine question about their process]**

That's it. The specific observation proves you looked. The question invites a response. No product, no pitch, no ask beyond the question itself.

**Test it:** Read the message back. If you could send it to a different builder by changing the company name, it's a template. Rewrite it.

### Step 4: After They Accept (the follow-up)
**Don't immediately pitch.** What you do depends on what happened:
- **They accepted AND replied to your question** → Have the conversation. The tool comes up naturally. Use the Tinkerer Intro when it fits.
- **They accepted but DIDN'T reply** → Send a short follow-up that stays in their world. Reference something new and specific (a recent post, a community update). No product yet. You're building familiarity.
- **They replied with useful info about their process** → NOW you can introduce the tool, grounded in what they just told you. "That's actually why I built this — [specific connection to what they said]."

The follow-up message (when you do introduce the tool):
- Lead with what it does, not what it's called
- Ground it in a real builder relationship ("started with a builder in Alabama")
- End with a soft ask ("happy to show you a quick example if you're curious")
- Never say "AI" — the tech is invisible

### Example: What Good Looks Like

**Bad (template):**
> "Hi [Name] — I've built a tool that helps builders visualize upgrade selections. Would love to show you how it works."

**Good (researched):**
> "Hi Mary — I actually came across your work with the design studio at Viera — winning the Nationals Silver for that is no small thing. Curious how the selection experience works at McKinley now that you're scaling across five states. Are buyers getting any kind of preview before the design appointment, or is it still mostly in-person?"

The difference: the bad one is about you. The good one is about her, grounded in something real from her career, and asks a question she'd actually want to answer.
